Key Readings

A curated, annotated guide to the most important books, papers, and resources — from first principles to the research frontier

L2

L3

L4

Suggested Reading Paths

Before diving into individual entries, here are three structured reading paths depending on your goal. Each path is self-contained — you do not need to follow all three.

Path A — Building Solid Foundations (6–8 weeks)

  • 1
    Faraway (2016) — Extending the Linear Model with R
    Weeks 1–2. Establishes GLMs, binary regression, count models with worked R examples. Most accessible starting point.
  • 2
    McCullagh & Nelder (1989) — Generalised Linear Models, Ch 1–5
    Weeks 3–4. The rigorous treatment of exponential families, IWLS, deviance, and model checking. Read alongside Faraway.
  • 3
    Burnham & Anderson (2002) — Model Selection and Multimodel Inference, Ch 1–4
    Weeks 5–6. AIC philosophy, how to use it in practice, what consistency means, and why BIC differs.
  • 4
    Pinheiro & Bates (2000) — Mixed-Effects Models in S and S-PLUS, Ch 1–3
    Weeks 7–8. Linear and nonlinear mixed models from first principles, with extensive R code.

Path B — Rigorous Theory Track (graduate course)

  • 1
    Akaike (1974) — original AIC paper
    Read first. Short (8 pages), foundational, illuminates the KL-divergence derivation.
  • 2
    Claeskens & Hjort (2008) — Model Selection and Model Averaging
    Full asymptotic theory, focused information criteria, model averaging. Read Ch 1–4 then dip into specific topics.
  • 3
    Dempster, Laird & Rubin (1977) — original EM paper
    Read the full paper. The derivation, the monotone-ascent proof, and all the examples are in 37 pages.
  • 4
    McLachlan & Krishnan (2007) — The EM Algorithm and Extensions
    The complete theory of EM, convergence, ECM/ECME variants, and applications.

Path C — Applied / Data Science Track

  • 1
    Gelman et al. (2020) — Regression and Other Stories
    The most readable modern treatment. Covers LM, GLM, multilevel models, causal inference — all with Stan/R examples.
  • 2
    Bates et al. (2015) — lme4 paper + package documentation
    Learn the computational details of lme4, its model syntax, and how to interpret output.
  • 3
    Davidian & Giltinan (1995) — Nonlinear Models for Repeated Measurement Data
    Focus on Ch 3–5 for NLME applications in pharmacokinetics and biology.
  • 4
    Fraley & Raftery (2002) — mclust paper
    Practical guide to model-based clustering with mclust in R.

Foundations — Probability & Inference

L1
Extending the Linear Model with R
Julian J. Faraway  ·  2016 (2nd ed.), CRC Press
Essential Applied GLMs Mixed models R code

What it covers: A superb first-graduate-level text. Starts with binary response models (logistic, probit), moves through count regression (Poisson, Negative Binomial, zero-inflated), multinomial and ordinal responses, then linear mixed models, GLMMs, and survival analysis. Every chapter has worked R examples with complete code.

Why read it: Faraway writes with exceptional clarity. Concepts are introduced through real datasets before any general theory, making motivation natural. By the end of this book you will be able to confidently fit and interpret any GLM or mixed model in R.

  • Chapter 1–3: Binary and binomial regression — logistic, probit, exact inference
  • Chapter 4–5: Count data — Poisson, Negative Binomial, overdispersion
  • Chapter 8: Linear mixed models with lme4
  • Chapter 9–10: GLMMs and GEE
Verdict: Start here if you are new to the material. The best bridge from undergraduate statistics to the content of this course. Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆
L2
Generalised Linear Models (2nd ed.)
Peter McCullagh & John A. Nelder  ·  1989, Chapman & Hall
Essential Theory Exponential family IWLS Deviance Canonical text

What it covers: The definitive mathematical treatment of GLMs. This book defines the exponential family, derives IWLS, establishes the deviance as a goodness-of-fit measure, and discusses model diagnostics at a depth unmatched elsewhere.

Why read it: If you want to truly understand why logistic regression uses the logit link, why Poisson regression uses the log, and what "canonical link" means mathematically, this is the source. The proof that IWLS is equivalent to Newton–Raphson on the log-likelihood is here and nowhere else at this level of clarity.

  • Chapter 2: The exponential family — the most important chapter
  • Chapter 3: Estimation: IWLS, score equations, Fisher information
  • Chapter 4: Inference: deviance, likelihood ratio tests, Wald tests
  • Chapter 5: Binary data — the full treatment of logistic/probit regression
  • Chapter 6: Multinomial and polytomous data

Key result to understand: The variance function $V(\mu) = b''(\theta)$ completely characterises the random component of a GLM. The link function is separate from the variance function — this is the central insight of the GLM framework.

Verdict: The canonical reference. Dense but precise. Read Chapters 2–4 before any other serious GLM work. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L2
An Introduction to Statistical Learning (2nd ed.)
James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani  ·  2021, Springer (free PDF)
Applied Cross-validation Model selection R code Free online

What it covers: Linear and logistic regression, model selection, cross-validation, regularisation (ridge, lasso), tree methods, SVM, and clustering. Available free at statlearning.com.

Why read it: Chapter 5 (Resampling: CV and bootstrap) and Chapter 6 (Linear Model Selection and Regularisation) are outstanding companions to Chapter 1 of these notes. The bias–variance tradeoff figures are among the clearest in the literature.

  • Chapter 5: Cross-validation — LOO, k-fold, bootstrap (perfectly complements §1.5)
  • Chapter 6: Subset selection, ridge, lasso — bridges to regularised model selection
Verdict: Very readable; best for CV and regularisation. Not a substitute for rigorous theory but excellent for applied intuition. Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆

Model Selection & Information Criteria

L4
A new look at the statistical model identification
Hirotugu Akaike  ·  1974, IEEE Trans. Automatic Control 19(6): 716–723
Essential paper Theory AIC origin KL divergence 8 pages

What it covers: The original AIC paper. Akaike begins from information theory (KL divergence between the true distribution $g$ and a parametric family $f_\theta$), notes that maximum likelihood asymptotically minimises the KL divergence, then derives the bias correction term that yields AIC. The entire argument fits in 8 pages.

Why read it: Understanding the original derivation is transformative. The key insight — that maximised log-likelihood is an upwardly biased estimator of predictive log-likelihood, with bias $\approx p$ — is simple once you see it, but took decades for the field to absorb fully.

Core equation to understand:

$E_g[\ell(\hat\theta;\mathbf{Y}^*)] \approx E_g[\ell(\hat\theta;\mathbf{Y})] - p$

where $\mathbf{Y}^*$ is new data. The AIC correction subtracts $p$ from the right-hand side to estimate the left. The factor of 2 is for compatibility with chi-squared tables.

Verdict: Required reading. 8 pages, freely accessible, one of the most cited papers in statistics. Read it at least twice. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L4
Estimating the dimension of a model
Gideon Schwarz  ·  1978, Annals of Statistics 6(2): 461–464
Essential paper Theory BIC origin Laplace approx 4 pages

What it covers: The original BIC paper — remarkably just 4 pages. Schwarz derives BIC from a Bayesian perspective: the $p\log n$ penalty arises from a Laplace approximation to the log marginal likelihood $\log p(\mathbf{y}|\mathcal{M})$. The derivation is essentially Theorem 1.4 of these notes, carried out in two paragraphs.

Key insight: The $\log n$ factor comes from the fact that $|J_n| = O(n^p)$ (the observed information grows as $n^p$), so $\frac{1}{2}\log|J_n| = \frac{p}{2}\log n + O(1)$. This is why BIC penalises complexity more as the sample size grows.

What it does NOT cover: BIC's finite-sample performance, how to choose between AIC and BIC, or any application. For those, see Burnham & Anderson (2002).

Verdict: Read immediately after the AIC paper. Together they take under 30 minutes and give you the complete foundational theory. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L2
Model Selection and Multimodel Inference: A Practical Information-Theoretic Approach (2nd ed.)
Kenneth P. Burnham & David R. Anderson  ·  2002, Springer
Essential Applied AIC in practice Model averaging Ecology

What it covers: A complete applied guide to AIC-based model selection. Covers $\Delta$AIC, AIC weights, model averaging, evidence ratios, and $\mathrm{AIC}_c$. Includes extensive examples from ecological research, but the principles are universal.

Key practical rules extracted from this book:

  • $\Delta\mathrm{AIC} < 2$: substantial support for this model
  • $\Delta\mathrm{AIC}$ between 4 and 7: considerably less support
  • $\Delta\mathrm{AIC} > 10$: essentially no support
  • AIC weights $w_i = \exp(-\Delta_i/2) / \sum_j \exp(-\Delta_j/2)$ — use for model averaging

Chapter 4 on multimodel inference (model averaging and evidence synthesis) is especially useful and goes beyond what most textbooks cover.

Verdict: The practitioner's bible for AIC. Opinionated (the authors are firmly anti-null-hypothesis testing) but enormously useful. Read Ch 1–4 as a priority. Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆
L3
Model Selection and Model Averaging
Gerda Claeskens & Nils Lid Hjort  ·  2008, Cambridge University Press
Theory Advanced FIC Asymptotic theory Focused selection

What it covers: The most mathematically complete treatment of model selection. Introduces the Focused Information Criterion (FIC) — the idea that the optimal model depends on what you want to estimate, not just predictive accuracy.

Key insight — FIC: AIC and BIC are omnibus criteria — they score models without regard to your specific inferential goal. FIC says: if you care about estimating $\psi = g(\boldsymbol{\beta})$ (e.g., a specific quantile or odds ratio), minimise the estimated MSE of $\hat\psi$ under each candidate model. Different quantities of interest may lead to different optimal models.

  • Chapter 2: Exact AIC and BIC derivations (the most precise treatment available)
  • Chapter 3: The Akaike process and TIC under misspecification
  • Chapter 5: Focused Information Criterion (FIC) — new concept not in these notes
  • Chapter 7: Frequentist model averaging theory
Verdict: The graduate student's model selection reference. Read after Burnham & Anderson for depth. Essential for anyone doing PhD-level work on model uncertainty. Difficulty: ★★★★☆
L4
Bayes Factors
Robert E. Kass & Adrian E. Raftery  ·  1995, JASA 90(430): 773–795
Essential paper Bayesian Bayes factors BIC connection 23 pages

What it covers: The definitive review of Bayes factors — what they are, how to compute them (including the BIC approximation), the interpretive scale (the table in §1.6 of these notes comes from here), and their relationship to $p$-values.

Key result: For large $n$, $2\log B_{10} \approx \mathrm{BIC}_0 - \mathrm{BIC}_1$, connecting Bayesian model comparison directly to BIC differences. This is the deepest reason BIC is Bayesian while AIC is frequentist.

The Lindley paradox (§1.6 of these notes) is explained clearly in §3.2 of this paper, with a concrete numerical example that makes the paradox vivid.

Section 5 — Intrinsic Bayes Factors: A solution to the improper-prior problem for Bayes factors, introduced here for the first time. Highly recommended.

Verdict: Read sections 1–4 carefully after studying §1.6 of these notes. Transforms your understanding of what BIC actually means. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆

Mixed Models & Longitudinal Data

L2
Mixed-Effects Models in S and S-PLUS
José C. Pinheiro & Douglas M. Bates  ·  2000, Springer
Essential Applied LMM NLME REML nlme package

What it covers: The companion book to the nlme R package. Part I covers linear mixed models exhaustively: model specification, REML, BLUPs, diagnostics, and correlation structures. Part II covers nonlinear mixed effects models with pharmacokinetic examples. The Rat growth and Theophylline examples in these notes come directly from this book.

Particularly important sections:

  • §2.2 — Model specification: How to formulate the $\mathbf{X}_i\boldsymbol{\beta} + \mathbf{Z}_i\mathbf{b}_i$ structure
  • §2.4 — REML: Why REML reduces bias in variance component estimation (most careful treatment available)
  • §2.5 — BLUPs: Full derivation of the BLUP formula and the Stein shrinkage interpretation
  • §6 — Covariance structure: AR(1), compound symmetry, unstructured — when to use each
  • §7 — NLME: Model building for pharmacokinetic data

Key formula highlighted: The BLUP $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b\mathbf{Z}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}(\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{X}_i\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})$ is derived from scratch with full motivation in Chapter 2.

Verdict: The most important single reference for Chapters 2–3 of these notes. Read Part I before fitting any LMM in a research context. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L2
Fitting Linear Mixed-Effects Models Using lme4
Douglas Bates, Martin Mächler, Ben Bolker & Steve Walker  ·  2015, Journal of Statistical Software 67(1): 1–48
Essential Applied R / lme4 Sparse matrices Free PDF

What it covers: The technical paper behind the lme4 package — the workhorse of mixed model fitting in R. Explains the Cholesky-based sparse matrix representation, the profiled log-likelihood optimisation, how REML is implemented, and how GLMMs are approximated via Laplace/AGQ.

What every practitioner must know from this paper:

  • Why REML=TRUE is the default and when to override it
  • How nAGQ controls quadrature accuracy (set to ≥ 10 for GLMMs)
  • Why lmer gives no $p$-values by default (the Satterthwaite/Kenward–Roger controversy)
  • How to read the summary() output — especially the correlation of fixed effects

Related resource: The lme4 vignette vignette("lmer", package="lme4") is a gentler companion that demonstrates the formula syntax with detailed examples.

Verdict: Required reading before using lme4 in research. Takes 2 hours; saves weeks of confusion. Freely available from JSS. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L4
Random-Effects Models for Longitudinal Data
Nan M. Laird & James H. Ware  ·  1982, Biometrics 38(4): 963–974
Landmark paper Theory LMM origin REML EM for LMMs

What it covers: The paper that introduced the LMM as we know it today. Defines the model, proposes both ML and REML estimation, uses the EM algorithm for computation, and derives the BLUP equations.

Historical significance: Before this paper, random effects models existed but had no general framework. Laird & Ware unified balanced/unbalanced designs, multiple random effects, and missing data under one model.

Key result: Under REML, the EM M-step for the variance components uses both the BLUPs $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i$ and their posterior covariance matrix $\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{b}_i|\mathbf{y}_i)$ — a subtlety often missed by practitioners implementing EM for LMMs from scratch.

Verdict: Read after Pinheiro & Bates for historical context and the original EM derivation for LMMs. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L4
Approximate Inference in Generalized Linear Mixed Models
Norman E. Breslow & David G. Clayton  ·  1993, JASA 88(421): 9–25
Essential paper Theory GLMM Laplace approx PQL bias

What it covers: Introduces PQL (Penalised Quasi-Likelihood) and the Laplace approximation for GLMMs. Most importantly, demonstrates that PQL is biased for binary data and count data with small denominators. The bias in variance components can be severe — making this the definitive warning against PQL.

Key simulation result to remember: For a random-intercept logistic model with $\sigma_b^2 = 1$ and average cluster size 5, PQL underestimates $\sigma_b^2$ by roughly 30–40%. The Laplace approximation and GHQ with ≥ 10 nodes are much more accurate.

What this tells practitioners: Never use glmer(..., nAGQ=0) (which triggers PQL) for binary responses. Always use at least nAGQ=1 (Laplace) and ideally nAGQ=15 or higher for small clusters.

Verdict: Essential before trusting any GLMM output. Read the simulation section carefully and memorise the rule: PQL is unreliable for binary GLMMs. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆

The EM Algorithm & Latent Variables

L4
Maximum Likelihood from Incomplete Data via the EM Algorithm
Arthur P. Dempster, Nan M. Laird & Donald B. Rubin  ·  1977, JRSS-B 39(1): 1–38
Landmark paper Theory EM origin Monotone ascent 37 pages

What it covers: The original EM paper — one of the most cited papers in the history of statistics (~50,000 citations). Defines the E-step and M-step, proves that the observed-data log-likelihood is non-decreasing at every iteration, establishes convergence conditions, and applies EM to a remarkable variety of problems:

  • ML from grouped, censored, and truncated data
  • Finite mixture models
  • Variance components in normal mixed models
  • Factor analysis
  • Hyperparameter estimation in empirical Bayes

Key proof: The monotone-ascent property (Theorem 4.1 of these notes) appears in §2 of this paper. The crucial step — that $H(\theta;\theta') \leq H(\theta';\theta')$ by Jensen's inequality — is proved in one sentence and repays careful study.

Missing information principle: Section 4 derives $J = I_c - I_m$ and shows that EM converges slowly when the fraction of missing information is high. This has practical implications for when to prefer Newton–Raphson over EM.

Verdict: Read every word. This is one of those papers that rewards rereading multiple times — each time you understand something you missed before. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L3
The EM Algorithm and Extensions (2nd ed.)
Geoffrey J. McLachlan & Thriyambakam Krishnan  ·  2007, Wiley-Interscience
Essential Theory EM convergence ECM / ECME MCEM Applications

What it covers: The definitive book on EM. Chapters 1–3 give the fullest treatment of convergence theory available (global/local convergence, rate analysis, missing information). Chapters 4–6 cover important extensions:

  • ECM (Expectation–Conditional Maximisation): When the M-step has no closed form, replace it with multiple conditional maximisations
  • ECME: ECM with maximisations over the observed-data likelihood (not Q), giving faster convergence
  • MCEM (Monte Carlo EM): Replace the E-step expectation by a Monte Carlo average — essential when the conditional distribution has no closed form
  • SEM (Supplemented EM): Computes the observed information matrix $J(\hat\theta)$ from EM iterates, bypassing numerical second derivatives

Chapter 4 on mixture models is the most complete treatment of Gaussian mixture fitting, identifiability, and model selection for $K$ in one place.

Verdict: The comprehensive EM reference. Read Ch 1–3 for theory; dip into later chapters by application. Difficulty: ★★★★☆
L3
Finite Mixture Models
Geoffrey J. McLachlan & David Peel  ·  2000, Wiley
Essential Theory Identifiability Mixture of regressions HMM Model selection for K

What it covers: The standard reference on mixture models. Covers Gaussian, Poisson, and binomial mixtures; mixtures of regressions; hidden Markov models; and finite mixture discriminant analysis. Chapter 6 on selecting the number of components $K$ is especially important — it covers BIC, bootstrap LRT, ICL, and the limitations of each.

Non-regularity (Chapter 3): The most careful treatment of why standard likelihood theory fails for mixtures. Key results:

  • The log-likelihood is unbounded for normal mixtures (degenerate solutions)
  • The LRT for testing $K=1$ vs $K=2$ is not asymptotically chi-squared — the usual regularity conditions fail
  • Bootstrap LRT is the reliable approach for testing $K$

Chapter 2 on EM for mixtures provides the complete algorithmic details, including initialisation strategies and convergence diagnostics specific to the mixture problem.

Verdict: Read alongside the mclust paper (Fraley & Raftery, 2002) for the complete mixture model toolkit. Difficulty: ★★★★☆
L4
Model-Based Clustering, Discriminant Analysis, and Density Estimation
Chris Fraley & Adrian E. Raftery  ·  2002, JASA 97(458): 611–631
Applied R / mclust BIC for K Covariance structures Free PDF

What it covers: The methodology behind the mclust R package. Gaussian mixture models with 14 covariance structures (spherical/diagonal/full × equal/unequal volume/shape/orientation). BIC is used to select both $K$ and the covariance structure simultaneously.

The 14 covariance models explained:

  • EII: Spherical, equal volume — like K-means clustering
  • VII: Spherical, varying volume — clusters of different sizes
  • EEE: Ellipsoidal, equal volume/shape/orientation — clusters with same shape
  • VVV: Fully unconstrained — most flexible, most parameters

Practical guidance from the paper: BIC consistently outperforms other criteria (AIC, ICL) for selecting $K$ in the simulation studies presented. The authors recommend fitting all 14 models for $K = 1, \ldots, 9$ and reporting the BIC-best solution.

Verdict: 20 pages that will make you an expert mclust user. Read before any model-based clustering project. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆

Bayesian Inference

L2
Bayesian Data Analysis (3rd ed.)
Andrew Gelman, John B. Carlin, Hal S. Stern, David B. Dunson, Aki Vehtari & Donald B. Rubin  ·  2013, CRC Press (free PDF)
Essential Bayesian MCMC Hierarchical models Stan Free online

What it covers: The standard graduate-level Bayesian textbook. Covers single-parameter and multiparameter models, hierarchical models, model checking (posterior predictive checks), MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings, Gibbs, HMC), Stan, and practical workflow. Freely available at stat.columbia.edu/~gelman/book.

For these notes, focus on:

  • Chapter 5 — Hierarchical models: The Bayesian treatment of what Chapter 2 calls LMMs. Includes the derivation of the BLUP as the posterior mean under a normal prior — connecting Bayesian and frequentist perspectives.
  • Chapter 11 — Gibbs sampler: The algorithm used for Bayesian LMM inference (§2.4 of these notes). Full derivation from conditional conjugacy.
  • Chapter 6 — Model checking: Posterior predictive checks as an alternative to information criteria for model adequacy.

Key insight about LMMs (Chapter 5): The BLUP $\hat b_i = \sigma_b^2/(\sigma_b^2 + \sigma^2/n_i) \cdot \bar e_i$ is exactly the posterior mean of $b_i$ under the normal prior $b_i \sim N(0,\sigma_b^2)$ — a beautiful unification of Bayesian and frequentist mixed-model inference.

Verdict: If you read one Bayesian book, read this. The posterior predictive check framework in Ch 6 is invaluable for model assessment. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
L1
Regression and Other Stories
Andrew Gelman, Jennifer Hill & Aki Vehtari  ·  2020, Cambridge University Press
Applied Multilevel models Causal inference R / Stan Highly readable

What it covers: A ground-up treatment of regression from a modern, practical perspective. Starts with linear regression and builds through GLMs, multilevel models (LMMs from a Bayesian perspective), causal inference, and model evaluation. Every concept is motivated by real social science or public health data.

Why it stands out: Exceptional emphasis on the workflow of applied statistics — designing studies, checking assumptions, interpreting coefficients, communicating uncertainty. The chapter on "Understanding regression models" (Ch 12) is unlike anything in standard textbooks: it carefully distinguishes predictive, descriptive, and causal interpretations of the same fitted model.

  • Part 2 (Ch 9–12): Model building and checking — directly complements Chapter 1 of these notes
  • Part 4 (Ch 17–22): Multilevel/hierarchical models — the applied perspective on Chapter 2
Verdict: The most readable modern statistics textbook available. Read in parallel with the more theoretical entries. Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆

Nonlinear Models

L3
Nonlinear Regression
George A. F. Seber & C. J. Wild  ·  2003, Wiley-Interscience
Essential Theory NLS theory Profile likelihood Intrinsic curvature

What it covers: The definitive NLS textbook. Part I establishes the mathematical theory: consistency and asymptotic normality of the NLS estimator, the Gauss–Newton algorithm and its convergence, profile likelihood confidence regions, and the Beale–Bates measures of nonlinearity.

Why profile likelihood intervals matter (Chapter 6): For highly nonlinear models, the sampling distribution of $\hat\beta_k$ can be very non-Gaussian. Wald intervals $(\hat\beta_k \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{se}})$ can have terrible coverage. Profile likelihood intervals are asymmetric, reflecting the true shape of the sampling distribution, and are always preferred in nonlinear settings.

Intrinsic curvature (Chapter 7): Introduces geometric measures of how "nonlinear" a model actually is. If the intrinsic curvature is small, linear approximations (Wald inference, Gauss–Newton convergence) work well. If it is large, expect problems.

Verdict: The go-to reference when NLS goes wrong — slow convergence, wide confidence intervals, or poor residuals. Difficulty: ★★★★☆
L2
Nonlinear Models for Repeated Measurement Data
Marie Davidian & David M. Giltinan  ·  1995, Chapman and Hall
Applied NLME Pharmacokinetics Biology Two-stage methods

What it covers: A practitioner-oriented guide to NLME models, focusing on pharmacokinetic and biological growth applications. Introduces two-stage methods (fit each individual separately, then combine) as a conceptual gateway to the full NLME model. Chapter 5 on the Theophylline dataset is the clearest exposition of pharmacokinetic model building in the literature.

Two-stage method (Chapter 3): Stage 1 — fit NLS to each individual; Stage 2 — treat individual estimates as data and model their distribution. Simple to implement and gives excellent starting values for the full NLME.

  • Chapter 5: Full pharmacokinetic analysis of Theophylline — the example in §3.3 of these notes
  • Chapter 7: Assessing goodness of fit in NLME — population vs. individual residuals
Verdict: Best applied companion for Chapter 3. The Theophylline walkthrough alone justifies the read. Difficulty: ★★★☆☆
