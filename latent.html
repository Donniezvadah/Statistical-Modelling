Latent Variables

Modelling what cannot be directly observed — and computing with it efficiently

4.1 What Is a Latent Variable?

A latent variable $U$ is a random variable that is not directly observed but influences the distribution of the observed data $Y$. Latent variables arise in at least three distinct ways:

  1. Genuinely hidden phenomena. An underlying continuous trait (e.g., disease severity, intelligence) may manifest only as a binary or ordinal observation (e.g., sick/healthy, exam grade). The latent variable represents the unobservable continuous quantity.
  2. Theoretical constructs. Quantities such as "anxiety", "social capital", or "market sentiment" are not directly measurable. They are operationalised as latent variables that explain correlations among measurable indicators.
  3. Computational tools. Even when a quantity is in principle observable, treating it as latent can make computation tractable — as in data augmentation for MCMC or the EM algorithm for mixture models.
💼 Example 4.1 — Galaxy Velocities: Hidden Clusters

The galaxies dataset (Roeder 1990) contains velocities (km/s) of 82 galaxies from a redshift survey. A histogram of the velocities is distinctly multimodal, suggesting the galaxies belong to several distinct clusters (superclusters). The cluster membership is the latent variable.

Probit Regression as a Latent Variable Model

Binary regression has a natural latent variable interpretation. Suppose an individual $i$ has a latent propensity $U_i \sim \mathrm{N}(\mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}, 1)$. We observe only whether this propensity exceeds a threshold: $$Y_i = \mathbf{1}(U_i \geq 0)$$ Then: $$P(Y_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = P(U_i \geq 0) = P\!\left(Z \geq -\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}\right) = \Phi(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta})$$ where $Z \sim \mathrm{N}(0,1)$. This is exactly the probit model.

💡 Three binary regression models from one latent variable idea

Changing the latent variable distribution changes the binary regression model:

  • $U_i \sim \mathrm{N}(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}, 1)$ → Probit: link $= \Phi^{-1}$
  • $U_i \sim \mathrm{Logistic}(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}, 1)$ → Logistic regression: link $= \mathrm{logit}$
  • $U_i \sim \mathrm{Gumbel}(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}, 1)$ → Complementary log-log: link $= \log(-\log(1-\cdot))$

All three give similar fitted probabilities in the middle of the range; they differ mainly in the tails.

4.3 Finite Mixture Models

A finite mixture model assumes the observed data $Y$ come from one of $K$ component distributions, where the component identity $U \in \{1, \ldots, K\}$ is latent.

📖 Definition 4.1 — Finite Mixture Model

The density of a $K$-component finite mixture is:

$$f(y;\, \boldsymbol{\pi}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k\, f_k(y;\, \theta_k)$$

where:

  • $\pi_k \geq 0$, $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$ — mixing weights (prior probabilities of component $k$)
  • $f_k(y;\theta_k)$ — component density with parameter $\theta_k$
  • $U \sim \mathrm{Categorical}(\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_K)$ is the latent component label
  • $Y \mid U = k \sim f_k(\cdot;\theta_k)$

Common Mixture Specifications

NameComponents $f_k$Parameters $\theta_k$
Gaussian mixture $\mathrm{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$ $(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$
Poisson mixture $\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_k)$ $\lambda_k$
Mixture of regressions $\mathrm{N}(\mathbf{x}^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}_k, \sigma_k^2)$ $(\boldsymbol{\beta}_k, \sigma_k^2)$

Non-Regularity Issues

Finite mixture models suffer from identifiability problems that make standard MLE theory break down:

  1. Label switching: Permuting the component labels gives the same likelihood. The mixture $\pi_1 f_1 + \pi_2 f_2$ is identical to $\pi_2 f_2 + \pi_1 f_1$. This creates $K!$ equivalent global maxima.
  2. Degenerate components: For Gaussian mixtures, setting $\sigma_k^2 \to 0$ and $\mu_k = y_i$ for a single observation makes $f_k(y_i;\theta_k) \to \infty$, so the likelihood is unbounded. The global MLE does not exist without constraints.
  3. Redundant components: Setting $\pi_k = 0$ removes component $k$ entirely, making $\theta_k$ non-identifiable.
⚠️ Practical consequences

Always constrain $\sigma_k^2 \geq \sigma^2_{\min} > 0$ to prevent degeneracy. Use multiple random restarts to explore the likelihood surface. Standard likelihood ratio tests for the number of components $K$ are invalid (the regularity conditions of Theorem 1.2 do not hold here). Use BIC or bootstrap likelihood ratio tests instead.

4.4 The Expectation–Maximisation Algorithm

The EM algorithm (Dempster, Laird and Rubin, 1977) is a general-purpose tool for computing MLEs when the data are incomplete — either literally (missing observations) or conceptually (the latent variable $U$ is unobserved). It is one of the most widely used algorithms in statistics and machine learning.

Setup: Complete vs. Observed Data

Let $\mathbf{y}$ be the observed data and $\mathbf{u}$ be the missing/latent data. Together $(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{u})$ constitute the complete data. Define:

  • $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \log f(\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})$ — observed-data log-likelihood (what we want to maximise)
  • $\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \log f(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{u};\boldsymbol{\theta})$ — complete-data log-likelihood (easier to maximise)

The key identity linking them:

$$\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log f(\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

Deriving the EM Algorithm from First Principles

Take the expectation of both sides with respect to $f(\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{y}; \boldsymbol{\theta}')$, where $\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ is the current parameter estimate:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}\!\left[\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid \mathbf{y}\right] = \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}\!\left[\log f(\mathbf{U} \mid \mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid \mathbf{y}\right]$$

Define the two functions:

$$Q(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \boldsymbol{\theta}') = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}\!\left[\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid \mathbf{y}\right]$$ $$H(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \boldsymbol{\theta}') = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}\!\left[\log f(\mathbf{U} \mid \mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid \mathbf{y}\right]$$

So we always have:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = Q(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}') - H(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}')$$
📐 Theorem 4.1 — Monotone Ascent of EM

Let $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} Q(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$ be the EM update. Then:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)}) \geq \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$$

That is, the observed-data log-likelihood is non-decreasing at every EM iteration. Equality holds only at a fixed point.

Proof of Theorem 4.1 — EM never decreases the likelihood

Using $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = Q(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}') - H(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}')$:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)}) - \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$$ $$= \underbrace{\left[Q(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) - Q(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})\right]}_{\geq\, 0 \text{ (M-step)}} - \underbrace{\left[H(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) - H(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})\right]}_{\leq\, 0 \text{ (shown below)}}$$

The M-step guarantees the first bracket is $\geq 0$ by definition. For the second bracket, using the non-negativity of KL divergence:

$$H(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) - H(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$$ $$= \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}}\!\left[\log \frac{f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)})}{f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})}\Bigg|\mathbf{y}\right] \leq \log \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)})}{f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})}\right] = \log 1 = 0$$

by Jensen's inequality (log is concave). Therefore:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)}) - \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) \geq 0 - 0 = 0$$
□

The EM Algorithm

📐 EM Algorithm — Formal Statement
  1. Initialise $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(0)}$ (randomly or using method of moments).
  2. E-step (Expectation): Compute $Q(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}}\!\left[\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid \mathbf{y}\right]$
    This requires computing the conditional distribution $f(\mathbf{u}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$ and taking the expectation of the complete-data log-likelihood.
  3. M-step (Maximisation): $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} Q(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$
  4. Convergence check: Stop if $|\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)}) - \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})| < \epsilon$ or $\|\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)} - \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}\| < \delta$. Otherwise return to Step 2 with $t \leftarrow t+1$.

Convergence Properties

EM is guaranteed to converge to a stationary point (local maximum, saddle point, or global maximum) of $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, but not necessarily the global maximum.

Rate of convergence: EM converges linearly (not quadratically like Newton's method). The rate is governed by the fraction of missing information:

$$r = \text{largest eigenvalue of } \mathcal{I}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})^{-1} \mathcal{I}_m(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$$

where $\mathcal{I}$ is the observed information and $\mathcal{I}_m$ is the "missing" information. When $r \approx 1$ (nearly all information is missing), EM is very slow. When $r \approx 0$ (little information is missing), EM is fast.

The Missing Information Principle

📐 Theorem 4.2 — Missing Information Principle

At any $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$\underbrace{J(\boldsymbol{\theta})}_{\text{observed info}} = \underbrace{\mathcal{I}_c(\boldsymbol{\theta})}_{\text{complete-data info}} - \underbrace{\mathcal{I}_m(\boldsymbol{\theta})}_{\text{missing info}}$$

where $\mathcal{I}_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\mathbb{E}[\partial^2\ell_c/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2 \mid \mathbf{y}]$ and $\mathcal{I}_m(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\mathbb{E}[\partial^2 \log f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2 \mid \mathbf{y}]$.

Proof of the Missing Information Principle

Differentiate the identity $\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log f(\mathbf{u}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})$ twice with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \ell_c}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2} = \frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \log f(\mathbf{u}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2}$$

Take the conditional expectation given $\mathbf{y}$ under $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial^2\ell_c}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2}\Big|\mathbf{y}\right] = \frac{\partial^2\ell}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2} + \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log f(\mathbf{U}\mid\mathbf{y};\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}^2}\Big|\mathbf{y}\right]$$

Multiply through by $-1$:

$$\mathcal{I}_c(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \mathcal{I}_m(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

Rearranging gives the result.

4.5 EM for Gaussian Mixture Models

The most famous application of EM: fitting a $K$-component Gaussian mixture. Let $Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \overset{\mathrm{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \, \mathrm{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$. The latent variables are the component labels $U_i \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$.

Complete-Data Log-Likelihood

Introduce indicator variables $Z_{ik} = \mathbf{1}(U_i = k)$. The complete data is $\{(y_i, z_{i1}, \ldots, z_{iK})\}_{i=1}^n$. The complete-data log-likelihood factors as:

$$\ell_c(\boldsymbol{\pi}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2) = \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{k=1}^K Z_{ik} \left[\log\pi_k + \log\phi(y_i;\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)\right]$$

where $\phi(y;\mu,\sigma^2) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/2}\exp(-(y-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2))$.

E-Step: Computing Responsibilities

The E-step replaces each $Z_{ik}$ by its conditional expectation given $\mathbf{y}$ and current parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)} = (\boldsymbol{\pi}^{(t)}, \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(t)}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^{2(t)})$:

$$w_{ik}^{(t)} = \mathbb{E}[Z_{ik} \mid y_i; \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}] = P(U_i = k \mid y_i; \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$$

By Bayes' theorem:

$$\boxed{w_{ik}^{(t)} = \frac{\pi_k^{(t)}\, \phi(y_i;\mu_k^{(t)},\sigma_k^{2(t)})}{\sum_{j=1}^K \pi_j^{(t)}\, \phi(y_i;\mu_j^{(t)},\sigma_j^{2(t)})}}$$

These are called responsibilities: $w_{ik}^{(t)}$ is the probability that observation $y_i$ was generated by component $k$, given the current parameters.

M-Step: Updating Parameters

Maximise $Q(\boldsymbol{\theta};\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}) = \sum_i\sum_k w_{ik}^{(t)}[\log\pi_k + \log\phi(y_i;\mu_k,\sigma_k^2)]$ over $(\boldsymbol{\pi}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2)$.

📐 Theorem 4.3 — M-Step Closed-Form Updates for Gaussian Mixtures

The M-step solutions are:

$$\pi_k^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n w_{ik}^{(t)} \qquad \text{(weighted proportion in component } k\text{)}$$ $$\mu_k^{(t+1)} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w_{ik}^{(t)} y_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n w_{ik}^{(t)}} \qquad \text{(responsibility-weighted mean)}$$ $$\sigma_k^{2(t+1)} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w_{ik}^{(t)} (y_i - \mu_k^{(t+1)})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n w_{ik}^{(t)}} \qquad \text{(responsibility-weighted variance)}$$
Proof of Theorem 4.3

Mixing weights: Maximise $\sum_k (\sum_i w_{ik})\log\pi_k$ subject to $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$ using a Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\pi_k}\left[\sum_j n_j \log\pi_j - \lambda(\sum_j\pi_j - 1)\right] = \frac{n_k}{\pi_k} - \lambda = 0$$

where $n_k = \sum_i w_{ik}$. So $\pi_k = n_k/\lambda$. Since $\sum_k\pi_k=1$, we get $\lambda = n$, giving $\pi_k = n_k/n$.

Means: Differentiate $\sum_i w_{ik}\log\phi(y_i;\mu_k,\sigma_k^2) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma_k^2}\sum_i w_{ik}(y_i-\mu_k)^2 + \mathrm{const}$ with respect to $\mu_k$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu_k} = \frac{1}{\sigma_k^2}\sum_i w_{ik}(y_i - \mu_k) = 0 \implies \mu_k = \frac{\sum_i w_{ik} y_i}{\sum_i w_{ik}}$$

Variances: Differentiate with respect to $\sigma_k^2$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\sigma_k^2} = -\frac{n_k}{2\sigma_k^2} + \frac{1}{2\sigma_k^4}\sum_i w_{ik}(y_i-\mu_k)^2 = 0 \implies \sigma_k^2 = \frac{\sum_i w_{ik}(y_i-\mu_k)^2}{n_k}$$
□
💡 Beautiful interpretation of EM for mixtures

EM for Gaussian mixtures alternates between two intuitive steps:

  • E-step: "Given these component parameters, how likely is each observation to belong to each component?" → Update responsibilities $w_{ik}$.
  • M-step: "Given these soft cluster assignments, what are the best component parameters?" → Update $\mu_k$, $\sigma_k^2$, $\pi_k$ as weighted statistics.

This is a soft version of K-means clustering. K-means assigns each point to exactly one cluster (hard assignment); EM assigns fractional responsibilities (soft assignment).

💼 Example 4.2 — EM for Gaussian Mixture (from scratch in R)
R
# ── Implement EM for a 2-component Gaussian mixture ──────────────
em_gaussian_mixture <- function(y, K = 2, max_iter = 200, tol = 1e-8) {
  n <- length(y)

  # Initialise: random responsibilities
  set.seed(42)
  w <- matrix(runif(n * K), nrow = n, ncol = K)
  w <- w / rowSums(w)

  params <- list()
  loglik_trace <- numeric(max_iter)

  for (iter in seq_len(max_iter)) {
    # ── M-step ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    nk  <- colSums(w)                          # effective cluster sizes
    pi  <- nk / n                              # mixing weights
    mu  <- colSums(w * y) / nk                 # weighted means
    sig2 <- sapply(1:K, function(k)            # weighted variances
      sum(w[,k] * (y - mu[k])^2) / nk[k])

    # ── E-step ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    dens <- sapply(1:K, function(k) pi[k] * dnorm(y, mu[k], sqrt(sig2[k])))
    total_dens <- rowSums(dens)
    w <- dens / total_dens                     # responsibilities

    # ── Log-likelihood ──────────────────────────────────────────
    loglik_trace[iter] <- sum(log(total_dens))

    # ── Convergence ─────────────────────────────────────────────
    if (iter > 1 && abs(loglik_trace[iter] - loglik_trace[iter-1]) < tol) {
      loglik_trace <- loglik_trace[1:iter]
      break
    }
  }

  list(pi = pi, mu = mu, sigma = sqrt(sig2), w = w,
       loglik = loglik_trace, n_iter = length(loglik_trace))
}

# Apply to galaxy data
library(MASS)
data(galaxies)
fit_em2 <- em_gaussian_mixture(galaxies, K = 2)
fit_em4 <- em_gaussian_mixture(galaxies, K = 4)

cat("2-component EM: mu =", round(fit_em2$mu), "\n")
cat("4-component EM: mu =", round(fit_em4$mu), "\n")

# Compare via BIC (manually)
bic_k <- function(fit, y, K) {
  n <- length(y); p <- 3*K - 1  # K means, K variances, K-1 free weights
  -2 * tail(fit$loglik, 1) + p * log(n)
}
cat("BIC K=2:", bic_k(fit_em2, galaxies, 2), "\n")
cat("BIC K=4:", bic_k(fit_em4, galaxies, 4), "\n")

4.6 EM for Exponential Family Complete Data

When the complete-data density belongs to an exponential family, the EM algorithm takes a particularly elegant and unified form.

📖 Definition 4.2 — Exponential Family Complete Data

Suppose $\log f(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u};\boldsymbol{\theta})$ has the form:

$$\log f(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u};\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbf{s}(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u})^\top \boldsymbol{\theta} - \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + c(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u})$$

where $\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u})$ is the complete-data sufficient statistic and $\kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the log-partition function.

📐 Theorem 4.4 — EM in Exponential Families

For exponential family complete data:

  • E-step: Compute $\mathbf{s}^{(t)} = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}}[\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{U}) \mid \mathbf{y}]$ (replace the complete-data sufficient statistic by its conditional expectation)
  • M-step: Solve $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbf{s}^{(t)}$ (match expected sufficient statistics to their natural values)

This means we can reuse any existing complete-data fitting routine, simply replacing $\mathbf{s}$ by its conditional expectation $\mathbf{s}^{(t)}$.

💼 Example 4.3 — Negative Binomial via EM

The Negative Binomial arises as a Poisson–Gamma mixture: $Y \mid U \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(U)$ and $U \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\nu, \nu/\mu)$. The complete data is $(y, u)$.

Complete-data log-likelihood:

$$\ell_c(\mu, \nu) = y\log u - u - \log y! + \nu\log(\nu/\mu) + (\nu-1)\log u - \nu u/\mu - \log\Gamma(\nu)$$

E-step: We need $\mathbb{E}[U \mid Y=y]$ and $\mathbb{E}[\log U \mid Y=y]$. Since $U \mid Y=y \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(y+\nu, (1+\nu/\mu)^{-1})$:

$$\mathbb{E}[U \mid y] = \frac{y + \nu}{1 + \nu/\mu} = \frac{\mu(y+\nu)}{\mu + \nu}$$ $$\mathbb{E}[\log U \mid y] = \psi(y+\nu) - \log(1 + \nu/\mu)$$

where $\psi$ is the digamma function.

M-step: Update $\mu^{(t+1)}$ by differentiating $Q$ w.r.t. $\mu$:

$$\mu^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[U_i \mid y_i; \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\mu^{(t)}(y_i + \nu^{(t)})}{\mu^{(t)} + \nu^{(t)}}$$
R
# EM for Negative Binomial (fixed nu, update mu only for illustration)
em_negbin_mu <- function(y, nu, mu_init = mean(y), max_iter = 100) {
  mu <- mu_init
  for (t in seq_len(max_iter)) {
    # E-step: E[U|y]
    eu <- mu * (y + nu) / (mu + nu)
    # M-step: mu = mean of E[U|y]
    mu_new <- mean(eu)
    if (abs(mu_new - mu) < 1e-10) break
    mu <- mu_new
  }
  mu
}

# Generate data
set.seed(1)
y <- rnbinom(200, size = 3, mu = 10)
cat("True mu = 10, EM estimate:", em_negbin_mu(y, nu = 3), "\n")
cat("Direct MLE (mean):", mean(y), "\n")

4.7 Data Augmentation and Bayesian EM

The EM philosophy extends naturally to Bayesian computation via data augmentation (Tanner and Wong, 1987). Instead of maximising the posterior, we sample from it using a Gibbs sampler that alternates between:

  1. Augmentation step (A-step): Sample the missing data $\mathbf{u}^{(t)} \sim f(\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{y}, \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$ (analogous to the E-step, but sampling rather than taking expectations).
  2. Posterior step (P-step): Sample the parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)} \sim \pi(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{u}^{(t)})$ (analogous to the M-step, but sampling from the complete-data posterior).

The resulting chain $\{(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}, \mathbf{u}^{(t)})\}$ converges to samples from the joint posterior $\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{y})$. Marginalising over $\mathbf{u}$ gives samples from $\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{y})$.

Chapter Summary

Key Points from Chapter 4

  • A latent variable is unobserved but influences the data distribution. It may represent a hidden quantity, a theoretical construct, or a computational device.
  • Finite mixture models express the marginal density as a weighted sum of component densities. The latent variable is the component label $U_i \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$.
  • The EM algorithm iterates between an E-step (replace latent data by conditional expectations) and an M-step (maximise the expected complete-data log-likelihood). It is guaranteed to non-decrease $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ at every iteration.
  • For Gaussian mixtures, the E-step computes responsibilities $w_{ik}$ and the M-step gives weighted sample statistics in closed form.
  • For exponential family complete data, EM reduces to replacing sufficient statistics by their conditional expectations — allowing reuse of standard fitting routines.
  • Data augmentation is the Bayesian analogue: sample rather than compute expectations, producing full posterior distributions.
