Statistical Modelling

A complete, beginner-to-expert guide — from first principles to modern methods

Welcome

"All models are wrong; the practical question is how wrong do they have to be to not be useful." — George E. P. Box (1979)

These notes form a complete course in Statistical Modelling, designed to take you from the foundations of probability and likelihood all the way to advanced topics such as mixed models, nonlinear regression, and latent variable methods. Whether you are encountering these ideas for the first time or refreshing knowledge you built elsewhere, every section is written to be self-contained, mathematically rigorous, and rich with intuitive explanations.

The guiding philosophy is that understanding the why behind every formula matters just as much as knowing the formula itself. Wherever a result can be derived, we derive it from first principles — step by step. Wherever a concept can be illustrated, we illustrate it with real data or concrete numerical examples.

Prerequisites

These notes assume familiarity with the following topics. Do not worry if your knowledge is a little rusty — brief reminders are given wherever they are needed.

Probability

  • Random variables and their probability distributions (discrete and continuous)
  • Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions
  • Expectations, variances, and covariances
  • The multivariate normal distribution $\mathbf{Y} \sim \mathrm{N}_p(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$
  • Convergence in probability and in distribution; the central limit theorem

Statistical Inference

  • Maximum likelihood estimation and its asymptotic properties
  • The score function and observed / expected Fisher information
  • Likelihood ratio, Wald, and score tests
  • Bayesian updating: prior $\times$ likelihood $\propto$ posterior

Statistical Models

  • Linear regression: $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, ordinary least squares, residual diagnostics
  • Generalised linear models (GLMs): exponential family distributions, link functions, iteratively reweighted least squares (IWLS)
  • Basic R programming: data frames, glm(), lm(), plotting
📘 Tip for complete beginners

If GLMs are new to you, spend a few hours with any introductory statistics text covering logistic and Poisson regression first. Everything in these notes builds naturally on top of that foundation.

The Four Guiding Principles of Modelling

Before diving into the mathematics, it is worth grounding ourselves in four overarching principles that should guide every modelling decision you make. These will reappear throughout the course.

Principle 1 — Substantive Knowledge First

A statistical model should encode what you know before seeing the data. If you are modelling drug concentration in blood over time, a mechanistic pharmacokinetic equation is far better than a polynomial with arbitrarily many terms. Prior scientific knowledge reduces the effective model space and guards against spurious discoveries.

Principle 2 — Robustness

No model is exactly right. A good model should give approximately correct answers even when some of its assumptions are mildly violated. Chapter 2 develops quasi-likelihood methods and marginal models precisely because they relax distributional assumptions while retaining the interpretability of GLM-style inference.

Principle 3 — Quality of Fit

After fitting a model, always examine residuals and goodness-of-fit statistics. A model that passes all parsimony criteria but whose residuals show obvious structure is still wrong in a practically important way. Residual plots, Q–Q plots, and deviance residuals are your first diagnostic tools.

Principle 4 — Parsimony

Among models that fit equally well, prefer the simpler one. This is Occam's Razor, formalised in information criteria such as AIC and BIC. Simpler models generalise better to new data, are easier to interpret, and require fewer assumptions.

💡 The tension between fit and parsimony

Adding parameters to a model always improves in-sample fit. The question is whether the improvement justifies the added complexity. Chapter 1 makes this tension mathematically precise through the bias–variance decomposition and information criteria.

The Model Hierarchy

The four chapters trace a hierarchy of increasingly general models. The diagram below summarises how each model class extends the previous one:

📐 Model Hierarchy
Linear Models (LM)
  └─▶ Generalised Linear Models (GLM)
        └─▶ Generalised Linear Mixed Models (GLMM)   ← Chapter 2
              └─▶ Generalised Nonlinear Mixed Models (GNLMM)  ← Chapter 3
                    └─▶ Latent Variable Models (LVM)  ← Chapter 4
                          └─▶ Mixture Models (EM)     ← Chapter 4

A Linear Model (LM) assumes $Y_i = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon_i$ with $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathrm{N}(0,\sigma^2)$. A GLM replaces $\mathrm{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ by any exponential family distribution and introduces a link function $g$ so that $g(\mu_i) = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}$. A GLMM adds random effects $\mathbf{b}_i$ into the linear predictor; a GNLMM further replaces the linear predictor with a nonlinear function $\eta(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \mathbf{x}_i)$. Finally, Latent Variable Models provide a unifying framework for all of the above by explicitly modelling unobserved quantities.
