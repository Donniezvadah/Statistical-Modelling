Beyond Generalised Linear Models

Handling overdispersion, clustering, and unobserved heterogeneity

2.1 A Brief Review of GLMs

A Generalised Linear Model (GLM) consists of three components working together:

  1. A distributional assumption: $Y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i$ belongs to the exponential family with density $$f(y_i; \theta_i, \phi) = \exp\!\left\{\frac{y_i \theta_i - b(\theta_i)}{a(\phi)} + c(y_i,\phi)\right\}$$ The mean and variance are $\mu_i = b'(\theta_i)$ and $\mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = a(\phi) b''(\theta_i)$.
  2. A systematic component (linear predictor): $\eta_i = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}$, where $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a covariate vector and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is a parameter vector.
  3. A link function: $g(\mu_i) = \eta_i$, connecting mean to linear predictor. Common choices: logit for Bernoulli, log for Poisson, identity for Normal.

When GLM Assumptions Fail

GLMs can break down in four important ways:

ProblemSymptomChapter 2 Solution
Overdispersion Residual deviance $\gg$ degrees of freedom Quasi-likelihood, Beta-Binomial, Negative Binomial
Clustering / dependence Observations within groups are correlated Mixed models, GEE
Unobserved heterogeneity Unit-to-unit variation unexplained by covariates Random effects (GLMM)
Non-independence Spatial, temporal, or hierarchical data structure Marginal models with structured correlation

2.2 Overdispersion

Overdispersion occurs when the observed variance in the data exceeds the variance predicted by the assumed distribution. For example, the Poisson model requires $\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \mu$, but in practice the variance is often a multiple $\phi > 1$ of the mean.

📖 Definition 2.1 — Overdispersion

A response $Y$ exhibits overdispersion relative to a GLM if $$\mathrm{Var}(Y \mid \mathbf{x}) > V(\mu)/m$$ where $V(\mu) = b''(\theta)$ is the variance function of the exponential family and $m$ is the relevant denominator (e.g., binomial $m$ for proportions). The dispersion parameter is $\phi = \mathrm{Var}(Y) / (V(\mu)/m) > 1$.

💼 Example 2.1 — Toxoplasmosis Data

Data from 34 cities in El Salvador record the number of individuals testing positive for toxoplasmosis infection. The covariate is annual rainfall (mm). We model the proportion of positives as Binomial.

R
library(MASS)
data(Toxo)  # or load from your source

# Fit cubic logistic regression
fit_cubic <- glm(cbind(Cases, Total - Cases) ~ poly(Rainfall, 3),
                 family = binomial, data = Toxo)

# Diagnose overdispersion
cat("Residual deviance:", deviance(fit_cubic), "\n")
cat("Degrees of freedom:", df.residual(fit_cubic), "\n")
cat("Deviance/df ratio:", deviance(fit_cubic) / df.residual(fit_cubic), "\n")
# Ratio >> 1 signals overdispersion

The residual deviance of approximately 62.6 on 30 degrees of freedom (ratio $\approx 2.1$) is strong evidence of overdispersion. The binomial model is inadequate.

Quasi-Likelihood

The quasi-likelihood approach, introduced by Wedderburn (1974), relaxes the full distributional assumption. Instead of specifying $f(y;\boldsymbol{\theta})$, we only specify the mean–variance relationship: $$\mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i] = \mu_i, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i) = \phi \frac{V(\mu_i)}{m_i}$$

The quasi-score function is defined as:

$$U(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(y_i - \mu_i)}{\phi V(\mu_i)/m_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \mu_i}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}$$

This is exactly the score of a genuine exponential family model with the given mean–variance structure, even though no such distribution may exist. Parameter estimates are obtained by solving $U(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \mathbf{0}$.

📐 Theorem 2.1 — Consistency of Quasi-MLE

If the mean model $g(\mu_i) = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}$ and the variance function $\phi V(\mu_i)/m_i$ are correctly specified, the quasi-MLE $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is consistent and $$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} - \boldsymbol{\beta}_0) \overset{d}{\to} \mathrm{N}\!\left(\mathbf{0},\, \phi (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{W} \mathbf{X})^{-1}\right)$$ where $\mathbf{W} = \mathrm{diag}(m_i V(\mu_i)^{-1} (\partial\mu_i/\partial\eta_i)^2)$.

Why does quasi-likelihood work? (Proof sketch)

The key insight is that all the information we need about $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ from the data is captured by the first two moments. The quasi-score satisfies:

  • $\mathbb{E}[U(\boldsymbol{\beta}_0)] = \mathbf{0}$ (unbiasedness)
  • $\mathrm{Var}(U(\boldsymbol{\beta}_0)) = -\mathbb{E}[\partial U / \partial \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top]$ (information identity)

These two properties are sufficient for the standard M-estimator theory to apply, giving consistency and asymptotic normality even without a full likelihood.

□

In practice, fit a quasi-model in R by specifying family = quasibinomial or quasipoisson:

R
# Quasi-binomial adjusts for overdispersion automatically
fit_quasi <- glm(cbind(Cases, Total - Cases) ~ poly(Rainfall, 3),
                 family = quasibinomial, data = Toxo)

summary(fit_quasi)
# Standard errors are inflated by sqrt(phi_hat) relative to binomial

Beta-Binomial Model

The Beta-Binomial model provides a parametric account of overdispersion. The idea: the success probability $P_i$ itself varies randomly across units.

📖 Definition 2.2 — Beta-Binomial Model

Assume $P_i \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_i, \beta_i)$ with $\mu_i = \alpha_i/(\alpha_i + \beta_i)$ and precision $\rho_i = 1/(\alpha_i + \beta_i + 1) \in (0,1)$. Given $P_i$, responses are $Y_i \mid P_i \sim \mathrm{Bin}(m_i, P_i)$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_i] = m_i \mu_i, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = m_i \mu_i(1-\mu_i)\left[1 + (m_i-1)\rho_i\right]$$

The factor $[1 + (m_i-1)\rho_i] > 1$ is the overdispersion multiplier. The intraclass correlation $\rho_i$ measures the variability in $P_i$.

Derivation of Beta-Binomial mean and variance

Using the law of total expectation and variance:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_i] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid P_i]] = \mathbb{E}[m_i P_i] = m_i \mu_i$$ $$\mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(Y_i \mid P_i)] + \mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid P_i])$$ $$= \mathbb{E}[m_i P_i(1-P_i)] + \mathrm{Var}(m_i P_i)$$ $$= m_i[\mu_i(1-\mu_i) - \mathrm{Var}(P_i)] + m_i^2 \mathrm{Var}(P_i)$$

For $P_i \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)$, $\mathrm{Var}(P_i) = \mu_i(1-\mu_i)\rho_i$ where $\rho_i = 1/(\alpha+\beta+1)$. Substituting:

$$\mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = m_i\mu_i(1-\mu_i)(1-\rho_i) + m_i^2 \mu_i(1-\mu_i)\rho_i$$ $$= m_i\mu_i(1-\mu_i)[1 + (m_i-1)\rho_i]$$
□

Negative Binomial for Count Overdispersion

For count data (Poisson), the analogous model is the Negative Binomial. Let $U_i \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\nu, \nu/\mu_i)$ (so $\mathbb{E}[U_i] = \mu_i$, $\mathrm{Var}(U_i) = \mu_i^2/\nu$). Given $U_i$, $Y_i \mid U_i \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(U_i)$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_i] = \mu_i, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = \mu_i + \frac{\mu_i^2}{\nu}$$

As $\nu \to \infty$, the Poisson is recovered. The parameter $1/\nu$ is the overdispersion parameter. In R:

R
library(MASS)
# glm.nb fits Negative Binomial
fit_nb <- glm.nb(y ~ x1 + x2, data = mydata)
summary(fit_nb)
# Theta = nu (overdispersion parameter)

2.3 Dependence and Clustered Data

Many datasets have a clustered structure: observations within a group (cluster, subject, school, city) are correlated, while observations from different groups are independent.

💼 Example 2.2 — Rat Growth Data

Body weight (grams) of 30 rats measured at weeks 1, 3, 5, 7, and 9 after birth. Interest is in the growth trajectory. Individual rats vary in both their starting weight (intercept) and growth rate (slope).

R
data(Rats, package = "nlme")  # or equivalent

# Naive linear regression ignoring clustering
fit_naive <- lm(weight ~ week, data = Rats)

# Plot residuals by rat - reveals clustering structure
library(ggplot2)
Rats$resid <- residuals(fit_naive)
ggplot(Rats, aes(x = week, y = resid, group = rat, colour = factor(rat))) +
  geom_line(alpha = 0.6) +
  labs(title = "Residuals by rat — clear between-rat heterogeneity",
       x = "Week", y = "Residual")

The residual plot shows that observations from the same rat tend to be on the same side of zero — evidence of positive intraclass correlation. Ignoring this will underestimate standard errors and inflate false-positive rates.

Marginal vs. Subject-Specific Models

There is a fundamental conceptual distinction between two modelling approaches for clustered data:

Approach Parameter meaning Question answered
Marginal models (GEE) Population-averaged effect of $\mathbf{x}$ "What happens to the average response if we change $\mathbf{x}$?"
Mixed / random-effects models Subject-specific effect of $\mathbf{x}$ "For a specific individual with given random effects, how does response change with $\mathbf{x}$?"

For linear models these two coincide (marginalising over random effects does not change regression coefficients). For nonlinear models (e.g., logistic regression) they give different values and the distinction is crucial.

Generalised Estimating Equations (GEE)

GEE, proposed by Liang and Zeger (1986), extends quasi-likelihood to clustered data. Let $Y_{ij}$ be the $j$-th observation in cluster $i$ ($j = 1, \ldots, n_i$; $i = 1, \ldots, m$). The GEE approach specifies:

  • $\mathbb{E}[Y_{ij}] = \mu_{ij}$ with $g(\mu_{ij}) = \mathbf{x}_{ij}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}$
  • $\mathrm{Var}(Y_{ij}) = \phi V(\mu_{ij})/w_{ij}$
  • A working correlation matrix $\mathbf{R}_i(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ for observations within cluster $i$

The GEE estimating equations are:

$$\sum_{i=1}^m \mathbf{D}_i^\top \mathbf{V}_i^{-1}(\mathbf{y}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i) = \mathbf{0}$$

where $\mathbf{D}_i = \partial \boldsymbol{\mu}_i / \partial \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top$ and $\mathbf{V}_i = \phi \mathbf{A}_i^{1/2} \mathbf{R}_i(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) \mathbf{A}_i^{1/2}$ is the working covariance matrix ($\mathbf{A}_i = \mathrm{diag}(V(\mu_{ij})/w_{ij})$).

📐 Theorem 2.2 — Robustness of GEE

If the mean model $g(\mu_{ij}) = \mathbf{x}_{ij}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}$ is correctly specified, the GEE estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is consistent regardless of whether the working correlation $\mathbf{R}_i$ is correct. Its asymptotic covariance is given by the sandwich estimator:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) \approx \mathbf{A}^{-1} \mathbf{B} \mathbf{A}^{-1}$$

where $\mathbf{A} = \sum_i \mathbf{D}_i^\top \mathbf{V}_i^{-1} \mathbf{D}_i$ and $\mathbf{B} = \sum_i \mathbf{D}_i^\top \mathbf{V}_i^{-1} \mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{y}_i) \mathbf{V}_i^{-1} \mathbf{D}_i$.

💡 Choosing a working correlation structure

Although consistency holds for any working correlation, efficiency improves when the working correlation approximates the true one. Common choices:

  • Independence: $\mathbf{R}_i = \mathbf{I}$ — ignores clustering (equivalent to standard GLM with robust SE)
  • Exchangeable: $\mathrm{Cor}(Y_{ij}, Y_{ik}) = \alpha$ — appropriate for cross-sectional clusters
  • AR(1): $\mathrm{Cor}(Y_{ij}, Y_{ik}) = \alpha^{|j-k|}$ — appropriate for time series
  • Unstructured: all correlations estimated freely — flexible but requires large $m$

2.4 Linear Mixed Models (LMMs)

A Linear Mixed Model includes both fixed effects (population-level parameters) and random effects (cluster-specific deviations treated as random draws from a distribution).

📖 Definition 2.3 — Linear Mixed Model

For observations $Y_{ij}$ ($j = 1,\ldots,n_i$; $i = 1,\ldots,m$):

$$Y_{ij} = \mathbf{x}_{ij}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + \mathbf{z}_{ij}^\top \mathbf{b}_i + \varepsilon_{ij}$$

where:

  • $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ — fixed-effects parameter vector (population average)
  • $\mathbf{b}_i \sim \mathrm{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b)$ — random effects for cluster $i$
  • $\varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathrm{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ — within-cluster residual noise
  • $\mathbf{b}_i \perp \varepsilon_{ij}$ for all $i, j$

In matrix form: $\mathbf{Y}_i = \mathbf{X}_i \boldsymbol{\beta} + \mathbf{Z}_i \mathbf{b}_i + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i$

Marginal Distribution of $\mathbf{Y}_i$

By marginalising over the random effects $\mathbf{b}_i$:

$$\mathbf{Y}_i \sim \mathrm{N}\!\left(\mathbf{X}_i \boldsymbol{\beta},\; \underbrace{\mathbf{Z}_i \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b \mathbf{Z}_i^\top + \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}_{n_i}}_{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i}\right)$$

The marginal covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i$ has a structured form: observations within the same cluster are correlated through the random effects, while the $\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}$ term adds independent residual noise.

💼 Example 2.3 — Random Intercept Model

The simplest LMM: each cluster has a random intercept. $\mathbf{z}_{ij} = 1$, $b_i \sim \mathrm{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$:

$$Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + b_i + \beta_1 x_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}$$

The marginal covariance within cluster $i$ is:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(Y_{ij}, Y_{ik}) = \sigma_b^2, \quad \mathrm{Var}(Y_{ij}) = \sigma_b^2 + \sigma^2$$

The intraclass correlation (ICC) is $\rho = \sigma_b^2 / (\sigma_b^2 + \sigma^2)$. This measures the proportion of total variance attributable to cluster-level differences.

Random Effects vs. Fixed Effects

Why model cluster effects as random rather than as fixed dummy variables?

AspectFixed EffectsRandom Effects
Parameters One per cluster — $m$ extra parameters Two: $\sigma_b^2$ and $\sigma^2$
Generalisation Inference only about observed clusters Inference about the population of clusters
Singletons Cannot estimate effect for $n_i = 1$ Possible via shrinkage (BLUP)
Efficiency Low when $m$ is large Borrows strength across clusters (shrinkage)

Restricted Maximum Likelihood (REML)

Maximum likelihood estimation of variance components $(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b, \sigma^2)$ is biased because it does not account for degrees of freedom used in estimating $\boldsymbol{\beta}$. REML removes this bias by maximising the likelihood of a set of residual contrasts that are free of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$.

📖 Definition 2.4 — REML Log-Likelihood

Let $\mathbf{A}$ be an $(n - p) \times n$ matrix with rows spanning $\{\mathbf{c} : \mathbf{c}^\top \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{0}\}$ (residual contrasts). The REML log-likelihood is:

$$\ell_{\mathrm{REML}}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b, \sigma^2) = \ell_{\mathrm{ML}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\mathrm{GLS}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b, \sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2}\log|\mathbf{X}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{X}|$$

where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathrm{block\,diag}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i)$ is the full marginal covariance and $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\mathrm{GLS}}$ is the GLS estimator.

⚠️ Important: REML and model comparison

REML log-likelihoods can only be compared across models with the same fixed-effects structure. To compare models with different fixed effects, use ML (set REML = FALSE in lmer).

Best Linear Unbiased Prediction (BLUP)

Random effects $\mathbf{b}_i$ are not parameters (they are random), but we can predict them. The BLUP minimises mean squared prediction error among all linear predictors:

$$\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b \mathbf{Z}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i^{-1}(\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{X}_i\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})$$

This can be written in the illuminating form:

$$\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i = \left(\mathbf{Z}_i^\top \mathbf{Z}_i / \sigma^2 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b^{-1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{Z}_i^\top (\mathbf{y}_i - \mathbf{X}_i\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) / \sigma^2$$
💡 Shrinkage interpretation of BLUPs

The BLUP shrinks the cluster-specific OLS estimate toward zero (the prior mean). Clusters with few observations get shrunk more; clusters with many observations get shrunk less. This is the Stein shrinkage phenomenon — borrowing strength from the ensemble of clusters improves individual predictions.

Formally, if $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i^{\mathrm{OLS}}$ is the OLS estimate for cluster $i$ alone, then $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i = \mathbf{S}_i \hat{\mathbf{b}}_i^{\mathrm{OLS}}$ where $\mathbf{S}_i = (\mathbf{I} + \sigma^2(\mathbf{Z}_i^\top\mathbf{Z}_i)^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b^{-1})^{-1}$ is the shrinkage matrix.

💼 Example 2.4 — Rat Growth LMM
R
library(lme4)

# Load rat growth data (nlme package)
data(Rats, package = "nlme")

# Random intercept only
fit_ri <- lmer(weight ~ week + (1 | rat), data = Rats, REML = TRUE)

# Random intercept + random slope
fit_ris <- lmer(weight ~ week + (week | rat), data = Rats, REML = TRUE)

# Compare models (use ML for fair comparison)
fit_ri_ml  <- lmer(weight ~ week + (1 | rat),     data = Rats, REML = FALSE)
fit_ris_ml <- lmer(weight ~ week + (week | rat),  data = Rats, REML = FALSE)
anova(fit_ri_ml, fit_ris_ml)  # LRT

# Model summary
summary(fit_ris)

# Extract BLUPs
ranef(fit_ris)

# Plot predicted trajectories per rat
library(ggplot2)
Rats$fitted_ris <- fitted(fit_ris)
ggplot(Rats, aes(x = week, y = weight, group = rat)) +
  geom_line(aes(colour = factor(rat)), alpha = 0.4) +
  geom_line(aes(y = fitted_ris, colour = factor(rat)), linetype = 2) +
  labs(title = "Rat weights: observed (solid) and LMM-fitted (dashed)")

Bayesian Inference for LMMs — Gibbs Sampling

LMMs have a natural Bayesian interpretation: the random effects prior $\mathbf{b}_i \sim \mathrm{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b)$ is exactly a hierarchical prior. Bayesian inference uses MCMC to sample from the joint posterior $p(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \mathbf{b}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b, \sigma^2 \mid \mathbf{y})$.

With conjugate priors, the Gibbs sampler cycles through full conditional distributions in closed form:

  1. Update fixed effects: $\boldsymbol{\beta} \mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{b}, \sigma^2 \sim \mathrm{N}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\mathrm{GLS}}, \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1})$
  2. Update random effects: for each $i$, $\mathbf{b}_i \mid \mathbf{y}_i, \boldsymbol{\beta}, \sigma^2, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b \sim \mathrm{N}(\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i^{\mathrm{BLUP}}, \boldsymbol{\Lambda}_i^{-1})$ where $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_i = \mathbf{Z}_i^\top\mathbf{Z}_i/\sigma^2 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b^{-1}$.
  3. Update $\sigma^2$: $\sigma^2 \mid \mathbf{y}, \boldsymbol{\beta}, \mathbf{b} \sim \mathrm{Inv-Gamma}(a + n/2, b + \mathrm{RSS}/2)$
  4. Update $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b$: $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b^{-1} \mid \mathbf{b} \sim \mathrm{Wishart}(\nu_0 + m, (\Psi_0 + \sum_i \mathbf{b}_i \mathbf{b}_i^\top)^{-1})$

2.5 Generalised Linear Mixed Models (GLMMs)

GLMMs extend LMMs to non-normal responses (binary, count, etc.) by incorporating random effects into the GLM framework.

📖 Definition 2.5 — Generalised Linear Mixed Model (GLMM)

For observations $Y_{ij}$ in cluster $i$, with random effects $\mathbf{b}_i \sim \mathrm{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b)$:

$$Y_{ij} \mid \mathbf{b}_i \sim \mathrm{EF}(\mu_{ij}, \phi), \qquad g(\mu_{ij}) = \mathbf{x}_{ij}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + \mathbf{z}_{ij}^\top \mathbf{b}_i$$

where $g$ is a link function and EF denotes an exponential family distribution.

The GLMM Likelihood — Why Integration Matters

The marginal log-likelihood of the observed data requires integrating out the random effects:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \phi, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b) = \sum_{i=1}^m \log \int f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid \mathbf{b}_i; \boldsymbol{\beta}, \phi)\, \phi(\mathbf{b}_i; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b) \, d\mathbf{b}_i$$

For Gaussian $Y_{ij}$ this integral has a closed form. For Bernoulli or Poisson responses, it generally does not. Three computational strategies exist:

Gaussian–Hermite Quadrature

For a scalar random effect $b_i \sim \mathrm{N}(0,\sigma_b^2)$, change variables $u = b_i / (\sqrt{2}\sigma_b)$ and apply the Gauss–Hermite quadrature rule:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h(u) e^{-u^2} du \approx \sum_{q=1}^Q w_q h(t_q)$$

where $t_q$ are the $Q$ quadrature points (roots of the $Q$-th Hermite polynomial) and $w_q$ are the corresponding weights. This gives:

$$\int f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid b_i) \phi(b_i; \sigma_b^2) db_i \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \sum_{q=1}^Q w_q f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid b_i = \sqrt{2}\sigma_b t_q)$$
💡 How many quadrature points?

More quadrature points $Q$ gives higher accuracy but more computation. $Q = 20$–$25$ is usually sufficient for moderate cluster sizes. The accuracy degrades for large $\sigma_b$ (wide random effect distribution) and for higher-dimensional $\mathbf{b}_i$ (the curse of dimensionality).

Laplace Approximation

The Laplace approximation applies a second-order Taylor expansion of the log-integrand around its mode $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i = \arg\max_{\mathbf{b}} [f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid \mathbf{b}_i) \phi(\mathbf{b}_i)]$:

$$\log \int f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid \mathbf{b}) \phi(\mathbf{b}) d\mathbf{b} \approx \log f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid \hat{\mathbf{b}}_i) + \log \phi(\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i) + \frac{q}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\log |\mathbf{H}_i|$$

where $\mathbf{H}_i$ is the Hessian of $-\log[f(\mathbf{y}_i \mid \mathbf{b}) \phi(\mathbf{b})]$ evaluated at $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_i$, and $q = \dim(\mathbf{b}_i)$. The Laplace approximation is equivalent to 1-point Gaussian–Hermite quadrature at the mode.

Penalised Quasi-Likelihood (PQL)

PQL linearises the GLMM around current estimates of the random effects and iteratively fits an LMM. While fast and easy to implement, it is known to produce biased estimates — especially for binary data with small cluster sizes. It should be avoided in applications where accuracy matters.

💼 Example 2.5 — GLMM for Toxoplasmosis (Individual Level)

Model the binary infection status of individual $j$ in city $i$ with a random-intercept logistic model:

$$\mathrm{logit}(P(\mathrm{infected}_{ij} = 1 \mid b_i)) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathrm{Rainfall}_i + b_i, \qquad b_i \sim \mathrm{N}(0, \sigma_b^2)$$
R
library(lme4)

# Simulate individual-level toxoplasmosis data
# (or load from your dataset)
set.seed(123)
m <- 34       # cities
ni <- 50      # individuals per city
rainfall <- rnorm(m, 1800, 300)

b_true <- rnorm(m, 0, 1)
p <- plogis(-1 + 0.001 * rainfall[rep(1:m, each = ni)] +
              b_true[rep(1:m, each = ni)])
infected <- rbinom(m * ni, 1, p)
city_id  <- rep(1:m, each = ni)
toxo_df  <- data.frame(infected, rainfall = rainfall[city_id], city = factor(city_id))

# Fit GLMM with Gauss-Hermite quadrature (nAGQ > 1)
fit_glmm <- glmer(infected ~ scale(rainfall) + (1 | city),
                  data = toxo_df,
                  family = binomial,
                  nAGQ = 25)   # 25-point GHQ

summary(fit_glmm)
VarCorr(fit_glmm)   # random effect variance

Chapter Summary

Key Points from Chapter 2

  • Overdispersion (variance exceeds GLM prediction) can be handled by quasi-likelihood (adds dispersion parameter $\phi$) or by switching to Beta-Binomial / Negative Binomial distributions.
  • GEE provides population-averaged estimates robust to misspecification of the working correlation structure; uses sandwich SE.
  • LMMs decompose variance into between-cluster ($\sigma_b^2$) and within-cluster ($\sigma^2$) components. REML removes the bias in ML estimates of variance components.
  • BLUPs are the optimal linear predictors of random effects; they shrink cluster-specific estimates toward the population mean.
  • GLMMs extend LMMs to exponential family responses. The marginal likelihood requires integration over random effects, solved by Gauss–Hermite quadrature or the Laplace approximation.
